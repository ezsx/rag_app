## The techniques that frameworks can't configure for you

Standard frameworks like LlamaIndex and LangChain offer retrieval-and-generate pipelines with plug-in components. They handle the common case well. But your system's core problem — routing heterogeneous queries through specialized strategies — requires architectural decisions that live outside any framework's configuration surface. Here are the techniques with the strongest evidence, ranked by impact-to-effort ratio for your specific setup.

**Adaptive query routing** solves your pipeline's linearity directly. The Adaptive-RAG paper (Jeong et al., NAACL 2024) trains a lightweight classifier to bucket queries into three complexity levels: no retrieval needed, single-step retrieval, and multi-step iterative retrieval. With GPT-3.5 as the generator, Adaptive-RAG achieved **~50.7 F1** across single-hop and multi-hop QA benchmarks, outperforming fixed single-step retrieval (~48 F1) while being **3.5× faster** than always running multi-step (~51 F1). Even a FLAN-T5-Small classifier matched the XL variant's routing accuracy, proving the overhead is negligible. For your system, you don't need a trained classifier — Qwen3-30B-A3B in non-thinking mode can classify queries into your four categories (date filter, channel filter, entity lookup, broad semantic) via a structured prompt. Each category routes to a different Qdrant query: payload date-range filters, channel-name filters, named-entity extraction + exact match, or full hybrid BM25+dense retrieval. This alone turns your five ReAct tools from generic to specialized.

**CRAG (Corrective RAG)** adds self-correction after retrieval. Yan et al. (2024) introduced a three-way evaluator that scores each retrieved document as Correct, Incorrect, or Ambiguous, then triggers different actions: use the documents directly, discard and fall back to web search, or combine both. Using a fine-tuned T5-large evaluator (only 0.77B parameters), CRAG improved accuracy over standard RAG by **+7.0% on PopQA, +14.9% FactScore on biographies, and +36.6% on PubHealth**. The critical insight for your system: you already have ColBERT reranking scores. These scores can serve as your evaluator signal — set a threshold (e.g., ColBERT score < 0.3 → "Incorrect," 0.3–0.6 → "Ambiguous," > 0.6 → "Correct") to trigger corrective actions without training any new model. When retrieval quality is low, the agent can reformulate the query, broaden filters, or respond honestly that coverage is insufficient.

**Temporal query understanding** is non-negotiable for a news aggregator. ChronoQA (2025, Nature Scientific Data) analyzed 5,176 temporal QA questions over 300K+ news articles and found that **72% of incorrect RAG answers stem from retrieval failures** — the system simply couldn't find temporally relevant documents. TG-RAG (2025) showed that removing temporal retrieval drops accuracy from **0.599 to 0.382** — a 36% collapse. Your Telegram posts already have timestamps stored as Qdrant payloads. The implementation is straightforward: detect temporal expressions in queries ("last week," "after GPT-4o release," "recently"), normalize them to date ranges (regex for common patterns + Qwen3 fallback for ambiguous ones like "after the NVIDIA keynote"), and apply Qdrant's payload range filters. Add a recency decay factor to your RRF scoring — multiply by `exp(-λ × days_old)` — and temporal queries immediately become first-class citizens.

**Multi-source synthesis with contradiction detection** leverages your 36-channel advantage. Astute RAG (Wang et al., 2024, Google Cloud AI Research) introduced source-aware consolidation: tag each piece of information with its origin, iteratively resolve conflicts, and finalize based on reliability. It achieved **+6.85% average accuracy** over traditional RAG across TriviaQA, BioASQ, and PopQA, and was the only RAG method that matched LLM-without-RAG under worst-case scenarios (all retrieved passages unhelpful). For your system, this maps directly to channel-aware synthesis: when retrieved posts from different Telegram channels disagree on a claim, Qwen3 can explicitly flag the contradiction and attribute each position to its source channel. This is pure prompt engineering — no fine-tuning required — and it transforms your system from "search engine" to "analyst."

**Citation verification via NLI** provides the faithfulness guarantee that separates serious systems from demos. A DeBERTa-large model fine-tuned on MNLI (400M parameters, runs on CPU) can classify whether each generated claim is entailed by, neutral to, or contradicted by the retrieved documents. The RAGAS framework operationalizes this by decomposing answers into atomic claims and checking each against context, achieving higher correlation with human judgment than GPT-4 zero-shot scoring. With your short documents (100–1500 chars), NLI verification is computationally cheap — a few milliseconds per claim. XLM-RoBERTa-large-xnli handles Russian + English natively.

## The 2025–2026 agentic RAG landscape is moving fast

Several new patterns from 2025–2026 are directly applicable to your setup, and knowing them signals awareness of the frontier in interviews.

**Speculative RAG** (Wang et al., Google Research, ICLR 2025) is perhaps the most elegant new pattern. A smaller "drafter" model generates multiple answer candidates in parallel, each from a different subset of retrieved documents (clustered to minimize redundancy). A larger "verifier" then selects the best draft in a single pass. The results are striking: Speculative RAG with a 7B drafter + 8×7B verifier outperformed conventional RAG by **+12.97% on PubHealth** while cutting **latency by 50.83%**. This architecture was designed for exactly your hardware split — draft on the RTX 5060 Ti (fast consumer GPU), verify on the V100 (higher memory). Qwen3-30B-A3B's MoE architecture (3B active parameters) makes it an ideal drafter since compute cost matches a 3B model.

**Modular RAG** (Gao et al., 2024) provides the conceptual framework for what you're already building. It decomposes RAG into composable modules at three levels: stages (retrieval, generation, routing), sub-modules (query expansion, reranking), and atomic operators (embedding, LLM call, similarity computation). ComposeRAG (Wu et al., May 2025) proved the approach works: its modular pipeline with question decomposition, query rewriting, retrieval decision, and answer verification achieved **+15% accuracy over fine-tuning-based methods** and **reduced ungrounded answers by 10%**. This framing is powerful for interviews because it positions your custom pipeline as an instance of the modular RAG paradigm, not ad-hoc engineering.

**A-RAG** (February 2026) represents the latest frontier: a hierarchical retrieval interface that exposes three tools (keyword_search, semantic_search, chunk_read) to an LLM agent that autonomously decides search strategy. It outperformed GraphRAG, RAPTOR, LightRAG, and workflow RAG methods, and introduced a **test-time scaling law for RAG** — more compute at inference yields better results, analogous to scaling laws for training. The paper's key insight: "Neither single-shot retrieval nor predefined workflows are truly agentic."

**Context compression** deserves attention for inference efficiency. CORE (2025) uses reinforcement learning to train a Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct compressor that achieves **3.6% token compression ratio** while actually *improving* EM by 3.2 points on Natural Questions — outperforming RECOMP, LongLLMLingua, and NoiseFilter-IB by 4–5 EM points. This is "lossless" compression: better answers with fewer tokens. Running a 1.5B compressor on your RTX 5060 Ti before feeding context to Qwen3 on the V100 would reduce generation latency substantially.

**RAG-Fusion with diverse prompts** remains underrated. MQRF-RAG (2025, ACM) introduced four distinct query rewriting strategies and achieved **+14.45% Precision@5** on FreshQA and **+2.55% over standard RAG-Fusion** on HotPotQA. Hybrid+Diverse variants (RAG-Fusion with diverse prompts + BM25+vector fusion) showed **+22% NDCG@5 and +40% recall@10** over baseline. You already have RRF — extending it with LLM-generated query variants is a minimal-cost, high-return addition.

## What actually impresses in Applied LLM Engineer interviews

The gap between "I built a RAG" and "I designed a retrieval system" comes down to three capabilities that hiring managers probe relentlessly: **trade-off reasoning**, **measurement discipline**, and **production awareness**.

Anthropic's hiring philosophy is instructive: "We care about what you can do, not where you learned to do it. If you've done interesting independent research, written a thoughtful blog post, or contributed to open source, put that at the top of your resume." They explicitly value independent builders — and your self-hosted, framework-free system signals exactly that. The brain aneurysm detection work deployed in hospitals is a massive differentiator: it proves you've shipped production ML in a high-stakes, regulated environment. The interview narrative should be: "I've deployed production ML in healthcare, and I'm applying those same production principles to LLM systems."

**The ablation study is your secret weapon.** Most portfolio RAG projects show a final number and call it done. An ablation table that removes one component at a time — showing that ColBERT reranking contributes +X% relevancy, RRF adds +Y% over dense-only, the ReAct agent lifts multi-hop queries by +Z% — proves you understand *why* your system works, not just *that* it works. Structure it as a table with columns for configuration, answer relevancy, faithfulness, precision@5, and latency. Always include the full pipeline as baseline, then each "without X" variant, then a LlamaIndex/LangChain baseline for direct comparison. ICLR reviewers cite ablation studies as the single most common request in paper reviews — showing them in a portfolio project is a rare differentiator.

**Metrics beyond recall signal senior thinking.** Your current metrics (recall@5) are necessary but insufficient. The metrics that impress:

- **Faithfulness** (does the answer stay grounded in retrieved documents? Target: ≥0.92)
- **Latency breakdown** (retrieval time vs. reranking time vs. generation time — shows you profiled your pipeline)
- **Cost per query** (self-hosted compute cost vs. equivalent API cost — powerful for the self-hosted narrative)
- **Source diversity** (across your 36 channels — shows the system doesn't over-index one source)
- **Temporal relevance** (for news, uniquely important — are retrieved articles actually from the right time period?)

**The framework comparison table is your closer.** Build a simple LlamaIndex/LangChain baseline using their default retriever + reranker + generator chain, measure the same metrics, and present a side-by-side table. The narrative isn't "frameworks are bad" — it's "I evaluated frameworks and built custom where the tradeoffs justified it." This is exactly the build-vs-buy reasoning that senior engineers exhibit. From an analysis of custom vs. framework workflows: "Custom workflows demand more upfront thought... but this 'slowness' forces architectural clarity. The payoff shows — scaling, debugging, and compliance are far easier when the foundation is solid."

**Your self-hosted setup is a strength, not a limitation.** Frame it as cost engineering mastery: "Running Qwen3-30B-A3B locally costs ~$X/month in electricity versus ~$Y/month for equivalent API calls." Frame it as infrastructure depth: "I can tell you exactly how much VRAM ColBERT reranking uses, what the latency breakdown is at each pipeline stage, and what it costs per query. Most engineers who use OpenAI APIs can't tell you any of this." Frame it as production readiness: "No API rate limits, no service outages from third parties, no surprise pricing changes, deterministic outputs."

## The optimal 3–5 day battle plan

Given your current recall@5 of 0.61–0.76 (agent) and 0.73 (retrieval), here is the sequencing that maximizes both system quality and interview impact. Each day targets one major feature with compounding returns.

**Day 1: Query router + specialized tools** (highest single-day impact). Implement a prompt-based query classifier using Qwen3 in non-thinking mode. Classify incoming queries into five types: temporal ("what happened last week with..."), channel-specific ("what did @channel say about..."), entity lookup ("who is..."), multi-hop comparison ("compare what channels said about..."), and broad semantic search (default). For each type, create a specialized Qdrant query: temporal → payload date-range filter + semantic search; channel → payload channel filter + BM25; entity → exact match + dense fallback; comparison → multi-query decomposition + per-channel retrieval; broad → your existing hybrid pipeline. Wire each into a dedicated ReAct tool. This directly fixes the linear pipeline problem. Expected impact: **+8–15% recall on query-type-specific benchmarks**, particularly temporal and channel queries that currently perform worst.

**Day 2: Temporal normalization + CRAG-lite** (builds on Day 1's routing). Morning: implement temporal expression parsing — regex patterns for "last N days/weeks/months," "yesterday," "this week," plus Qwen3 fallback for ambiguous references ("after the DeepSeek release"). Add recency decay to RRF scoring. Afternoon: implement CRAG-lite using your existing ColBERT scores as the relevance evaluator. Define three thresholds: Correct (score > 0.6, use documents), Ambiguous (0.3–0.6, expand retrieval + use documents), Incorrect (< 0.3, reformulate query or report insufficient coverage). When triggered, the agent reformulates the query with different keywords or broader filters and retries once. Expected impact: **+10–20% on temporal queries**, **+5–10% overall from self-correction** on currently-failing queries.

**Day 3: LlamaIndex baseline + ablation study infrastructure** (interview impact day). Morning: build a LlamaIndex baseline — VectorStoreIndex with Qdrant, SentenceTransformerRerank, and a simple query engine. Use the same embedding model and Qdrant collection. Afternoon: design your evaluation suite. Use RAGAS for faithfulness and answer relevancy (reference-free, works with any corpus). Create 50–100 test queries manually across your five query types (10–20 per type) with ground-truth answers from your Telegram data. Run every pipeline variant: full pipeline, without ColBERT, without RRF, without ReAct agent, BM25-only, dense-only, LlamaIndex baseline. Record recall@5, precision@5, faithfulness, answer relevancy, and p50/p95 latency. Build the comparison table. This day produces the artifact that wins interviews.

**Day 4: Faithfulness verification + multi-source synthesis** (sophistication day). Morning: add NLI-based citation verification using XLM-RoBERTa-large-xnli (supports Russian + English). After Qwen3 generates an answer, decompose it into claims, check each against retrieved documents via NLI entailment scoring. Flag unsupported claims or add inline source attribution. This can run on CPU while the GPU handles generation. Afternoon: implement channel-aware synthesis for multi-source queries. When retrieved documents come from 3+ different channels, prompt Qwen3 to explicitly note agreements, disagreements, and information unique to each source. This leverages your 36-channel advantage as a feature, not just a data source.

**Day 5: Polish, documentation, and demo** (presentation day). Morning: run the full ablation study with all Day 1–4 additions. Update the comparison table. Write the README with architecture diagram, design decisions with justifications, ablation results, cost analysis (self-hosted vs. API), and known limitations. Afternoon: create a demo — either a Gradio/Streamlit interface or a recorded walkthrough showing each query type being handled differently. Show the system explicitly routing temporal queries through date filters, catching a low-confidence retrieval and self-correcting, and synthesizing contradictory information across channels. Commit with meaningful git history showing iterative development.

## The open-source arsenal you should leverage

The most valuable resources for your specific stack, ranked by direct applicability:

**For advanced RAG patterns**, NirDiamant/RAG_Techniques (24.7K GitHub stars) contains self-contained Jupyter notebooks implementing CRAG, Self-RAG, Adaptive RAG, Fusion RAG, and 18+ other techniques. Each notebook is framework-agnostic with clear code. This is your primary reference for implementation patterns. The official CRAG repository (HuskyInSalt/CRAG) and Self-RAG repository (AkariAsai/self-rag) provide the original paper implementations for deep reference.

**For query routing**, aurelio-labs/semantic-router (3.1K stars) is purpose-built for fast query classification using semantic vector space — no LLM call needed. It supports hybrid routing (dense + sparse), integrates with Qdrant natively, and its local models outperform GPT-3.5 at routing accuracy. This is the cleanest path to implementing your query type classifier without adding LLM latency on every query.

**For evaluation**, RAGAS (explodinggradients/ragas, ~7K stars) is the standard RAG evaluation framework with reference-free metrics: faithfulness, answer relevancy, context recall, and context precision. It integrates directly with Qdrant. For test data, MultiHop-RAG (Tang et al., 2024) provides a news-article-based benchmark with multi-hop queries requiring reasoning over multiple documents — nearly identical to your use case. Qdrant's own qdrant-rag-eval repository provides workshops comparing naive vs. hybrid RAG with RAGAS integration and ColBERT/Jina reranker comparisons.

**For Qdrant-specific patterns**, the BGE-M3 + Qdrant sample (yuniko-software/bge-m3-qdrant-sample) demonstrates generating dense, sparse, and ColBERT embeddings in a single model pass with Qdrant — exactly your retrieval stack. The Qdrant RAG optimization workshop uses HackerNews data (similar to your Telegram posts) with step-by-step optimization notebooks. The shanojpillai/qdrant-rag-pro repository implements production-ready patterns including hybrid search, metadata filtering, confidence scoring, and multilingual support with multilingual-e5.

**For custom ReAct agents**, Sinceru0/ReAct-Agent-from-Scratch provides a pure Python implementation with no framework dependencies — the cleanest starting point for understanding and extending the pattern. RAGent Chatbot (shafiqulai) combines ReAct + RAG + Qdrant hybrid search with web search fallback, closely mirroring your architecture.

**For Qwen3 optimization**, deploy with vLLM or SGLang for production inference (PagedAttention for efficient KV cache management, continuous batching for throughput). Use thinking/non-thinking mode switching strategically: non-thinking mode for query classification and simple generation (lower latency), thinking mode for complex multi-hop reasoning and synthesis. INT8 quantization on V100 gives minimal quality loss with significant memory savings.
